There was a time when multiplayer gaming didn't involve matchmaking queues, discord overlays, or fiber-optic connections. In the summer of 2001, multiplayer meant carrying a massive 17-inch CRT monitor (which weighed about as much as a small dog) and a beige desktop tower down to a friend's garage. 

We were there for one thing: **Diablo II**.

Opening that giant cardboard box for the first time is a memory burned into my mind. Inside was a thick, beautifully illustrated manual that felt like a spellbook, and a plastic multi-disc case containing the game. 

## The Sound of the Garage

A LAN party had a distinct soundscape:
* The rhythmic, heavy click-clack of membrane keyboards.
* The jet-engine roar of cheap PC case fans struggling in the summer heat.
* The periodic whirring of CD-ROM drives reading game assets.
* The frantic clicking of ball-mice (which you had to open and clean every few hours to keep them tracking smoothly).

We set up our tables, connected everything to a dusty 10Base-T hub, and shared IP addresses manually. There was no internet connection in that garage—just our local network, some bags of chips, and warm soda.

## Hunting for Loot in the Dark

We played until the sun came up. I was running a Necromancer, filling the screen with skeletons and causing lag that drove my friends crazy. When a unique item dropped, like a *Stone of Jordan*, the room would erupt. We didn't trade through online auction houses; we just negotiated across the table, trading items for a slice of pizza or a soda.

Looking back, those nights were magical. It wasn't about competitive ranks or battle passes. It was about the shared space, the physical presence of your friends, and the sheer wonder of exploring Sanctuary together through a glowing screen in a dark room.
